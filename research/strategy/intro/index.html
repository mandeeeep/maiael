Strategic Management Models Reference

Quick reference guide for all strategic management frameworks

1

External Environment Analysis

Understand your business environment, industry structure, and competitive landscape

External Factor Evaluation
EFE
Identifies and prioritizes external opportunities and threats affecting your organization

Key Components:

  • Opportunities - External factors that could benefit your organization
  • Threats - External factors that could harm your organization
  • Weights - Importance of each factor (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Ratings - How well your organization responds (1-4)
  • Weighted Scores - Weight × Rating for each factor

Key Questions:

What external factors could help or hurt us? How are we currently responding to these factors? What's our total weighted score?

Porter's Five Forces
5 FORCES
Analyzes industry structure and competitive intensity to determine industry attractiveness

The Five Forces:

  • Competitive Rivalry - Intensity of competition among existing firms
  • Threat of New Entrants - Ease/difficulty for new competitors to enter
  • Threat of Substitutes - Availability of alternative products/services
  • Bargaining Power of Buyers - Customer ability to negotiate prices
  • Bargaining Power of Suppliers - Supplier ability to influence prices

Key Questions:

How attractive is this industry? What forces drive competition and profitability? Where should we compete?

Competitive Profile Matrix
CPM
Benchmarks your organization against competitors on key success factors

Key Components:

  • Critical Success Factors - Key factors that determine success in the industry
  • Weights - Importance of each factor (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Ratings - Performance of each company on each factor (1-4)
  • Weighted Scores - Weight × Rating for each company
  • Comparison - Your score vs. competitors' scores

Key Questions:

How do we compare to our major competitors? What are our relative strengths and weaknesses?

2

Internal Capability Assessment

Understand your organization's strengths, weaknesses, and value creation activities

Porter's Value Chain
VC
Breaks down organization into activities to identify where value is created and costs are incurred

Primary Activities:

  • Inbound Logistics - Receiving, storing, distributing inputs
  • Operations - Transforming inputs into final products
  • Outbound Logistics - Collecting, storing, distributing products
  • Marketing & Sales - Providing means for buyers to purchase
  • Service - Enhancing/maintaining product value

Support Activities:

  • Firm Infrastructure - General management, planning, finance, legal
  • Human Resource Management - Recruiting, hiring, training, development
  • Technology Development - R&D, process automation, design
  • Procurement - Purchasing inputs used in value chain

Key Questions:

Where does our organization create value? What activities are we excellent at? What are our cost drivers?

3

Strategy Formulation

Decide where to compete and how to win

Vision & Mission Statements
V&M
Defines the organization's purpose, direction, and reason for being

Vision Statement:

  • Question: "What do we want to become?"
  • Purpose: Short, inspiring, reveals type of business
  • Example: "To be the world leader in transportation products and related services"

Mission Statement:

  • Question: "What is our business?"
  • Purpose: Declaration of organization's reason for being
  • Answer: Identifies customers, products, markets, technology, survival, philosophy, self-concept, public image, employees

The 9 Mission Components:

  • 1. Customers - Who are the firm's customers?
  • 2. Products/Services - What are the major products or services?
  • 3. Markets - Geographically, where does the firm compete?
  • 4. Technology - Is the firm technologically current?
  • 5. Survival/Growth/Profit - Is the firm committed to growth and financial soundness?
  • 6. Philosophy - What are the basic beliefs, values, aspirations, and ethical priorities?
  • 7. Self-Concept - What is the firm's major competitive advantage?
  • 8. Public Image - Is the firm responsive to social, community, and environmental concerns?
  • 9. Employees - Are employees a valuable asset of the firm?

Key Questions:

What do we want to become? What is our business? Who do we serve? What makes us different?

Ivey's 7 Steps
7 STEPS
Provides a comprehensive strategic planning process from problem definition to implementation

The Seven Steps:

  • Step 1: Define - Identify and clarify the problem or issue
  • Step 2: Gather Information - Collect external and internal data
  • Step 3: Generate Alternatives - Create multiple strategic options
  • Step 4: Evaluate Alternatives - Assess pros and cons of each option
  • Step 5: Decide - Select the best strategic alternative
  • Step 6: Implement - Execute the chosen strategy
  • Step 7: Follow Up - Review results and communicate outcomes

Key Questions:

What problem are we solving? What options do we have? Which one should we choose? How do we execute it?

Mintzberg's 5 Ps
5 Ps
Views strategy from five different perspectives to broaden understanding beyond planning

The Five Ps:

  • Plan - A deliberate, conscious course of action
  • Ploy - A maneuver to outwit competitors
  • Pattern - Consistent behavior that emerges over time
  • Position - Where the organization sits in its environment
  • Perspective - The collective mindset and approach of the organization

Key Questions:

Is strategy a plan, a ploy, a pattern, a position, or a perspective? How should we think about it?

Porter's Diamond Model
DIAMOND
Analyzes a nation's competitive advantage and factors that drive international competitiveness

The Four Main Components:

  • Factor Conditions - Nation's production resources (infrastructure, labor, natural resources, capital)
  • Demand Conditions - Nature of domestic market demand driving growth and innovation
  • Related & Supporting Industries - Presence of supplier industries and clusters
  • Strategy, Structure & Rivalry - How companies are created, organized, and managed; domestic competition

Two Additional Factors:

  • Government - Acts as catalyst and challenger to improve performance
  • Chance - External unpredictable events creating opportunities

Key Questions:

What gives our nation competitive advantage? How do the four determinants interact? What role does government play?

ACQR Criteria
ACQR
Evaluates strategic alternatives against key acceptance and feasibility criteria

The Five Criteria:

  • A - Acceptable - Is it politically and culturally acceptable to stakeholders?
  • C - Consistent - Is it consistent with firm's history, vision, and strategy?
  • Q - Qualitative/Quantitative - Can we measure it? Is ROI positive? Does the math make sense?
  • R - Resources - Do we have the required resources? What are the risks?
  • I - Implementation - Can we execute this strategy effectively?

Key Questions:

Is this strategy acceptable, consistent, measurable, feasible, and implementable?

Cross-Cutting Frameworks

These models apply throughout ALL phases of strategic management

Bloom's Taxonomy
TAXONOMY
Guides cognitive thinking depth from basic recall to creative strategic solutions

Six Cognitive Levels (Low to High):

  • Remember - Recall facts, terms, and basic concepts
  • Understand - Explain ideas or concepts in your own words
  • Apply - Use information in new situations
  • Analyze - Draw connections and break down information
  • Evaluate - Justify decisions based on criteria and evidence
  • Create - Produce new or original work and solutions

Application by Phase:

  • Phase 1 (External): Analyze external factors and their relationships
  • Phase 2 (Internal): Analyze value chain activities and connections
  • Phase 3 (Formulation): Evaluate alternatives and create strategic options
  • Phase 4 (Implementation): Apply understanding to implement effectively

Key Questions:

Are we just remembering information or are we analyzing, evaluating, and creating strategic solutions?

4

Strategy Implementation

Turn strategic decisions into action and ensure effective execution

Activity System Map
ASM
Visualizes how strategic activities reinforce each other to create competitive advantage

Key Components:

  • Strategic Theme - The central value proposition or positioning
  • Key Activities - Activities that directly support the theme
  • Supporting Activities - Activities that enable key activities
  • Connections - How activities reinforce each other
  • Fit - Coherence among all activities

Key Questions:

Do all our activities reinforce each other? Are we creating a coherent strategic system?
