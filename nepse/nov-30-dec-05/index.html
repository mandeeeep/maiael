Sustained Momentum: +61.01% ‚Üí +46.40% Despite Market Decline

Key Behavioral Factors:

Momentum Clustering
Extreme gains in week 1 (+61.01%) attracted sustained buying in week 2 (+46.40%), showing how momentum attracts momentum.
Contrarian Resilience
While market declined 1.68%, SAGAR surged 46.40%, demonstrating selective stock conviction overriding broader sentiment.

Investors assuming recent extreme performance will persist, forming mental shortcuts about stock behavior patterns.

Strong momentum creates self-reinforcing stories that attract new buyers regardless of fundamental developments.

Case Study: Monetary Policy Review Impact (Part 2)

Policy Paradox - Behavioral Analysis
Why Accommodative Policy Failed to Stimulate Credit Growth

The Policy Paradox Explained

Expected Response: Rate cuts and increased liquidity should stimulate borrowing and economic activity through cheaper credit costs.

Actual Response: Credit growth remained at 7.3% against 12% target, demonstrating behavioral factors can override monetary policy signals.

Root Cause: Rising NPLs (5.26% from 4.42%) creating institutional risk aversion among banks and borrowers.

Key Behavioral Factors:

Policy Paradox
Despite rate cuts and increased liquidity, banks maintain risk-averse lending behavior due to rising NPLs (5.26% from 4.42%), prioritizing asset quality over growth.
Sentiment vs. Fundamentals
Strong external position (BoP surplus: Rs 264B) not translating to domestic confidence, showing behavioral disconnect from macroeconomic data.
Institutional Risk Memory
Recent financial sector stress (including Prabhu Bank scandal) creating lasting risk aversion among financial institutions and borrowers.
Expectation Management
Market already priced in policy support, so actual announcements failed to generate significant positive sentiment despite substantive changes.

Behavioral Insight: Monetary policy effectiveness depends on behavioral factors - risk memory and sentiment can override policy signals, creating policy paradox where accommodative measures fail to stimulate credit growth.
