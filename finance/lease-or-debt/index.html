Lease Classification Creates Artificial Divide

Capital / Financial Leases

Treatment: Treated like an asset purchase financed with debt. Both the asset and corresponding liability appear on the balance sheet.

Criteria (FASB): A lease is "Capital" if it meets ONE of these four conditions:

  1. Lease term is ‚â• 75% of the asset's life
  2. Ownership transfers to the lessee
  3. Includes a 'bargain purchase' option
  4. Present value of payments is ‚â• 90% of the asset's value
Operating Leases

Treatment: Treated as a simple rental expense. Lease payments are listed on the income statement as operating expense, but the asset and long-term liability are absent from the balance sheet.

‚ö†Ô∏è Source of 'off-balance sheet financing'

Criteria:

Any lease that does not meet the criteria for a capital lease.

Key Insight: This distinction is driven primarily by who effectively owns the asset, not the nature of the financial commitment.

All Lease Commitments Are Debt

Supporting Arguments

What defines debt?
A contractually set claim where failure to pay has negative consequences.
Lease payments are fixed commitments
Just like bond coupons, they are specified at the start of the contract.
They are tax-deductible
Lease payments reduce taxable income, similar to interest expense.
Failure has consequences
Missing a lease payment can result in the loss of a critical operating asset (e.g., a store location).

Key Insight

The financial distinction between debt and equity is based on the nature of the cash flow claim. Leases are not residual claims; they are fixed claims. Therefore, they should be treated as debt in financial analysis, regardless of accounting rules.

"We should be doing what is right in valuation and corporate financial analysis, rather than bending our assessments to fit accounting rules that do not make sense."
‚Äì Aswath Damodaran

Ignoring Leases Distorts Profitability & Leverage

Return on Capital (ROC)

Effect: Operating Income is artificially lower (due to lease expense), and Capital is artificially lower (because leases are off-balance sheet).

Result: ROC is often misleadingly higher

Debt Ratio (Debt / Capital)

Effect: Debt does not include lease commitments, leading to understated Capital.

Result: The Debt Ratio is significantly lower, hiding the true leverage.

Interest Coverage (EBIT / Interest)

Effect: Operating Income is lower, and Interest expense does not include imputed interest from leases.

Result: The coverage ratio appears healthier than it is.

Four-Step Process to Uncover True Debt

1

Uncover Future Commitments

Extract all operating lease commitments from the footnotes of the financial statements (typically for the next 5 years and a lump sum beyond).

2

Determine the Cost of Debt

Estimate the firm's pre-tax cost of debt. Use the company's bond rating or a "synthetic" rating based on its interest coverage ratio.

3

Calculate the 'Debt Value' of Leases

Discount the future lease commitments back to the present using the pre-tax cost of debt as the discount rate. This present value is the debt equivalent of the leases.

4

Adjust Operating Income

Recalculate EBIT. Add back the operating lease expense (since it's a financing cost) and subtract the depreciation on the newly created 'leased asset'.

Formula for Adjusted EBIT

Adjusted EBIT = Stated EBIT + Operating Lease Expense - Depreciation on Leased Asset

Enterprise Value Multiples Built on Flawed Foundation

When operating leases are capitalized, both the numerator (Enterprise Value) and the denominator (EBITDA) change, altering key multiples used in relative valuation.

Critical Analysis

Comparing EV/EBITDA multiples across firms without adjusting for lease treatment is an apples-to-oranges comparison. The firm that leases its assets will almost always look deceptively cheaper.

Lease Capitalization Alters Intrinsic Value

Valuation is a function of cash flows, growth, and risk. Capitalizing leases affects every single input.

Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)

  • Operating Income increases
  • Net Capital Expenditures increase (to reflect changes in the PV of leases)

Cost of Capital (WACC)

  • Debt-to-Capital ratio increases significantly
  • Cost of Debt & Equity may change due to higher perceived leverage
  • Overall WACC generally decreases

Expected Growth (g = ROC * Reinvestment Rate)

  • Both ROC and the Reinvestment Rate are adjusted
  • The effect on sustainable growth is unpredictable

Final Impact

The net result of these shifts can dramatically increase or decrease the final estimated value per share.

The Analyst's Mandate

Key Points:

  1. Operating leases are debt in disguise, and standard accounting hides this reality.
  2. This distortion creates a false picture of leverage, profitability, and ultimately, value.
  3. A straightforward 4-step process allows us to correct for this distortion.
