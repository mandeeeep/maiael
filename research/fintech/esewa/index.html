1.0 Introduction: From Alignment to Fusion in the Digital Age

For decades, the prevailing view of technology strategy was that it was a functional component that needed to be aligned with business strategyâ€”a critical support player, but always subordinate. We have entered a new era where this model is obsolete. Business strategy and IT strategy are no longer merely aligned; they are fused into a single, overarching Digital Business Strategy. This fundamental paradigm shift moves technology from the back office to the absolute core of value creation and competitive advantage.

"The future of digital business strategy is already here, it is just unevenly distributed!"

â€” Bharadwaj et al., MIS Quarterly

The academic journal MIS Quarterly provides a working definition for this new paradigm, describing Digital Business Strategy as "an organizational strategy formulated and executed by leveraging digital resources to create differential value." This is not a siloed IT function but a pervasive, trans-functional approach that directly shapes a firm's market position. Digital resources become the connective tissue linking operations, marketing, and supply chains, enabling organizations to redefine their products, processes, and relationships with partners and customers.

The purpose of this framework is to introduce a powerful model for navigating this new era, built on four critical themes: Scope, Scale, Speed, and Sources of Value. To bring these principles to life, we will explore the remarkable journey of eSewa, Nepal's pioneering digital payments provider. The story of its rise from a simple startup to the dominant force in its nation's fintech landscape provides a masterclass in how these theoretical concepts translate into real-world market leadership and sustained growth.

1.1 Defining Digital Business Strategy

At its core, a Digital Business Strategy is defined as an "organizational strategy formulated and executed by leveraging digital resources to create differential value." This definition elevates digital technology from a supporting tool to the central engine of value creation, demanding a holistic fusion of business and technology at the highest level of corporate planning.

A fundamental paradigm shift is reshaping corporate strategy. For decades, Information Technology (IT) strategy was a functional-level activity, a subordinate process designed to support a pre-ordained business strategy. As articulated by Bharadwaj et al. in MIS Quarterly, this view is obsolete. In the modern era, the pervasive nature of digital resources has fused IT and business strategy into a single, indivisible concept: Digital Business Strategy. This analysis adopts this modern strategic framework to analyze the trajectory, competitive environment, and future options for eSewa, Nepal's pioneering fintech firm, which has navigated this fusion over its 16-year history.

2.1 Theme 1: Scope â€” Redefining Strategic Boundaries

In the digital age, the strategic importance of Scope is no longer confined to a company's portfolio of products or its linear supply chain. A digital business strategy fundamentally alters how we define an organization's boundaries, dissolving traditional industry silos and enabling the creation of dynamic, cross-industry ecosystems. Success is determined not just by what a firm owns, but by the network of partners, services, and customers it can effectively connect and coordinate.

Scope in a digital context is no longer defined by a firm's internal functions or industry classification. It expands in three critical ways: it becomes trans-functional, using digital platforms to integrate siloed operations; it includes the digitization of products and services, making the physical offering inseparable from its digital layer; and it extends beyond the firm's boundaries into dynamic ecosystems of partners and customers. For a firm like eSewa, this redefinition of Scope is critical; it means competitive advantage is no longer about having the most features in a single app, but about orchestrating the most valuable ecosystem of partners.

2.2 Theme 2: Scale â€” Achieving Exponential Growth

The concept of Scale has long been a driver of profitability, but its meaning has been radically redefined. In the industrial age, scale was achieved through capital-intensive assets: factories, physical distribution networks, and geographic footprint. In the digital age, scale is achieved through network effects, information abundance, and strategic partnerships, creating a dynamic capability for exponential and often asset-light growth.

Understanding these new levers of scale is paramount, as they allow digital-native firms to achieve market penetration and impact at a velocity and cost-structure that is impossible for traditional incumbents to match. A digital platform's value increases not with each factory built, but with each new user, partner, and service that joins its ecosystem.

2.3 Theme 3: Speed â€” Competing at Digital Velocity

In a digital business environment, Speed is not just a feature; it is a central driver of competitive advantage. Pervasive connectivity has recalibrated the clock-speed of entire industries. This manifests in accelerated product launches, faster and more data-driven decision-making, and the rapid formation of networks to sense and respond to market shifts. The ability to learn, adapt, and execute faster than competitors is fundamental to survival and success.

In the fintech arena, where market windows open and close with startling rapidity, the ability to execute with superior speed is a primary determinant of success or failure. eSewa's journey exemplifies this principle, having evolved its strategy through three distinct phases, each a rapid response to the maturing needs of the Nepalese market.

2.4 Theme 4: Sources of Value Creation and Capture

The ultimate objective of a digital business strategy is to architect new Sources of Value Creation and Capture. Digital business models unlock revenue streams that were previously unimaginable, deriving value from information, multi-sided platforms, and coordinated networks. Unlike traditional models, which often focus on a single transaction, digital platforms create ongoing value for multiple participants simultaneously.

Critically, firms that successfully control the key digital architecture of an industry can capture a disproportionate share of the profits. For eSewa, this means the future of profitability lies not just in transaction fees, but in its ability to own the central, indispensable platform within Nepal's financial ecosystem.

eSewa's Complete Strategic Journey from 2009 to Present

3.0 Case Study: How eSewa Built its Empire

eSewa's 16-year journey serves as a practical embodiment of the digital business strategy framework. Its market leadership was not accidental but the result of a phased, intuitive mastery of Scope, Scale, Speed, and Value Creation. This section deconstructs eSewa's historical phases to reveal the strategic logic that must now be deliberately reapplied to counter emerging threats.

3.1 The Birth of an Idea

The inspiration for eSewa stemmed from the founder's own direct experience with the "payment gap." While running a web domain business in the mid-2000s, co-founder Biswas Dhakal had no credit card and had to ask his US-based uncle to pay vendors for him. This personal frustration sparked the idea for a local digital payment solution. The solution began with Biswas Dhakal, a co-founder with a natural instinct for using technology to solve everyday problems.

"As a child, he watched his father, a lawyer working on the ground floor, have to shout up to the third-floor kitchen to request tea for guests. Dhakal's solution was to build a homemade intercom system. This innovative, problem-solving mindset would become the foundation of his future venture."

â€” Early Life of Biswas Dhakal, Co-founder

Years later, in the mid-2000s, Dhakal encountered a new kind of friction. While running a business selling website domains, he struggled to pay his US-based vendors because he didn't have a credit card. He often had to rely on an uncle in the US to make payments for him. This personal "payment gap" sparked the idea for eSewa (a name derived from the Nepali word for 'service'). Starting with an initial investment of just USD 134, Dhakal conceptualized a payment gateway for Nepal.

The idea gained crucial validation in 2007 when the CEO of Laxmi Bank, a commercial bank, invested USD 24,000, signaling that the vision had serious potential. But a visionary idea and initial funding were just the beginning; eSewa was about to face the immense realities of a market not yet ready for a digital revolution.

3.2 The Setting: Why Nepal Was Ready for Digital Change

Nepal is a land of incredible beauty, from its deep valleys to the majestic Himalayan peaks. However, this same geography creates significant barriers to infrastructure development, which has historically impacted access to critical services, including banking. Economically, many Nepalis face significant challenges. With a GDP per capita of approximately USD 1,460 by 2025, a large portion of the population has struggled with access to essential services and financial inclusion.

The Problem of Cash

In a country with difficult terrain and limited infrastructure, a cash-based economy can be incredibly inefficient and expensive for ordinary people. This reality is captured perfectly by Rupesh Neupane, Head of Business Development at eSewa, who recalled a neighbor's struggle:

"To pay a simple electricity bill of NPR 80 (approximately USD 0.59), his neighbor had to travel for two days and spend over NPR 1,000 (approximately USD 7.34) on transportation. The cost of paying the bill was more than twelve times the bill itself."

â€” Rupesh Neupane, Head of Business Development, eSewa

This was the everyday friction of a cash-based economy. The core problem was the sheer time and cost required for simple transactions. This landscape of high costs and logistical nightmares set the stage for a technological solution that promised to redefine Nepal's economy.

3.3 Phase 1: Financial Inclusion (2009-2017)

eSewa's path from a simple idea to a market leader can be understood as a strategic evolution through three distinct phases. As Jabir Ali, Head of Business Growth and Innovation, explained, the journey was a transition through "financial inclusion, move towards financial convenience, and is now transitioning to offer financial freedom to its users."

Phase 1

Financial Inclusion (2009-2017)

Overcoming the Barriers

When eSewa officially launched in 2009, it faced a trifecta of monumental challenges. The digital infrastructure and culture needed for its success simply did not exist.

  • Low Internet Penetration: In 2009, internet access in Nepal was a mere 2%. A digital service was of little use to a population that was overwhelmingly offline.
  • Lack of Smartphones: In an era before the smartphone boom, these devices were a "rare luxury," not the ubiquitous tools they are today.
  • A Cash-Centric Culture: Most importantly, people preferred cash. The concept of digital money was foreign, and there was a deep-seated aversion to adopting new payment methods.

The Agent Network: A Revolutionary Solution

To solve the adoption challenge, eSewa introduced its most critical innovation in 2012: the agent-led model. This system was revolutionary because it allowed customers to use eSewa's services without needing a smartphone, a bank account, or even the eSewa app.

The model was simple yet brilliant. eSewa onboarded a network of local agentsâ€”initially cyber cafes, but later expanding to mom-and-pop stores, pharmacies, and mobile repair shops. Customers could walk up to an agent, hand them cash, and have the agent perform a digital transaction on their behalf, from paying an electricity bill to recharging a mobile phone. These agents became more than just transaction points; they were trusted local guides who educated their communities about digital finance.

"Our agent network is what makes eSewa truly inclusive. We're not just for tech-savvy users in Kathmandu; we're for every Nepali, no matter where they live."

â€” Jagdish Khadka, CEO, eSewa

This agent-led model was a masterful manipulation of Scope and Scale. It expanded eSewa's operational Scope beyond firm boundaries into a dynamic physical-digital ecosystem, directly addressing a market segment inaccessible to purely digital competitors. By onboarding thousands of cyber cafes, pharmacies, and mom-and-pop stores, eSewa created a human interface for its digital services.

Concurrently, it achieved Scale not through digital network effects alone, but by leveraging alliances with these small businesses, creating a distributed physical presence that was prohibitively expensive for others to replicate. With agents handling approximately 85% of transactions initially, this hybrid model was the crucial bridge between Nepal's cash economy and its digital future.

Building Trust and Gaining Legitimacy

In its first several years, eSewa operated in a regulatory vacuum. With no formal rules governing digital wallets, the company had to self-regulate, establishing its own high standards for security, user verification, and transaction processing to build customer trust.

A pivotal moment came in 2017 when Nepal's central bank, Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB), introduced the official Payment Service Provider (PSP) license. This license provided a formal legal framework for the industry. eSewa became the first company, along with IME Pay, to receive the PSP license from the NRB, a landmark achievement that validated its security protocols and reinforced its credibility with millions of current and potential users.

3.4 Phase 2: Financial Convenience (2017-2020)

As technology adoption grew and new competitors emerged, eSewa shifted its strategy toward convenience. The goal was to transform its digital wallet into an integrated, "one-stop app" for everyday life. This was achieved through a series of strategic partnerships across key sectors.

Phase 2

Financial Convenience (2017-2020)

Creating a One-Stop App

eSewa aggressively broadened its Scope by forging strategic partnerships across diverse sectors, transforming its app into a "one-stop" platform. By integrating with over 50 banks, 4,500 schools, utility providers, and the travel industry, eSewa created a new Source of Value through a highly coordinated network.

  • Banks & Financial Institutions: Partnerships with over 50 banks allowed users to easily connect their bank accounts and use the "Load Money" feature to fund their eSewa wallets in real-time.
  • Utility Providers: Collaborations with electricity, water, and internet providers turned the tedious task of paying bills into a few simple taps on a screen.
  • Education: By integrating with over 4,500 schools and colleges, eSewa eliminated the need for parents and students to make in-person fee payments.
  • Travel & Hospitality: Partnerships with domestic airlines, bus operators, and over 1,500 hotels offered users a transparent and simple platform for booking travel.
  • Entertainment: Collaborations with major cinema chains made it easy for users to purchase movie tickets directly through the app.

This ecosystem generation created powerful lock-in effects; the convenience of managing all of life's payments through a single, integrated hub made switching to a less comprehensive competitor a significant downgrade for the user.

Building the Digital Infrastructure

The foundation of technology created the opportunity for eSewa's rise. The primary catalyst for Nepal's digital transformation was the dramatic increase in internet and smartphone access. The technological shift was swift and decisive, creating a fertile ground for digital innovation.

In its most recent phase, eSewa transitioned from a payment tool to a comprehensive financial ecosystem offering services like digital micro-lending, insurance, and remittances. This evolution represents a sophisticated strategy for Value Creation and Capture.

Part 3: Strategic Analysis

Competitive Landscape and Environmental Forces

4.0 The Current Competitive Landscape: A Market at Inflection Point

The fintech landscape in Nepal has transformed from a blue ocean pioneered by eSewa into a red ocean of intense competition, increasing regulatory pressure, and market consolidation. This new environment presents a critical challenge to eSewa's long-held dominance and marks a strategic inflection point for the company.

4.1 The Rules of the Game: Government Regulation

As digital payments grew, the government stepped in to create a formal, secure system. The country's central bank, Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB), took the lead. In 2015, the NRB established the Payment Systems Department (PSD). By 2017, it began issuing official licenses for two types of companies:

Payment Service Providers (PSPs)

Companies like digital wallets (e.g., eSewa) that provide payment services directly to customers.

Payment System Operators (PSOs)

Companies that manage the underlying payment network infrastructure.

For companies like eSewa, these new regulations had a dual impact, creating both opportunities and challenges:

Impact of Regulation on eSewa

  • Benefit - Building Trust: In 2017, eSewa became the first company, along with IME Pay, to receive the PSP license from the NRB. This official government endorsement validated the security of its platform, helping it gain the "trust of millions of users" and reinforcing its credibility as a reliable financial service.
  • Challenge - Increasing Costs: With the license came stricter rules. New requirements for Know Your Customer (KYC) verification, Anti-Money Laundering (AML) protocols, and transaction limits meant eSewa had to invest more in technology and processes. These changes resulted in "additional compliance costs" and could sometimes inconvenience users who needed higher transaction limits.

By formalizing the market and building public trust, these regulations legitimized the industry and inadvertently invited a wave of new entrants, intensifying the competitive pressure.

4.2 From Pioneer to Crowded Market

When eSewa first launched, "there were no other players" in the market. It was a true pioneer. Fast forward to 2025, and the landscape had completely transformed. Customers now had "25 digital wallets to choose from," creating a highly competitive environment.

In early 2025, the competitive landscape was shaken by the announcement that Khalti and IME Pay would merge. This move was set to create a "formidable rival with greater scale and comparable market share," posing the most direct and significant threat to eSewa's market leadership to date.

The strategic logic of the Khalti and IME Pay merger is clear: it creates a direct challenger by fusing complementary assets to expand Scope and Scale. The new entity will have an unparalleled Scope, covering Khalti's urban youth segment and IME Pay's rural remittance users. This combination immediately grants it a Scaleâ€”in both user base and agent networkâ€”that directly threatens eSewa's market leadership.

The merger is not merely an additive move; it is a multiplicative one, creating a full-spectrum competitor poised to challenge eSewa across every key market segment.

4.4 Market and Regulatory Pressures

eSewa's strategic decisions must also account for powerful external forces reshaping the industry:

External Pressures Facing eSewa

  • Intensifying Competition: With 25 licensed PSPs, the market is crowded, leading to aggressive price wars that are squeezing profit margins.
  • Regulatory Headwinds: Stricter policies from Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) on transaction limits and capital requirements are increasing operational complexity and compliance costs.
  • Looming Global Threats: The potential market entry of giants like Google Pay and Alipay poses a significant disruptive threat that could fundamentally alter the competitive landscape.
  • Low Customer Switching Costs: In a market with minimal product differentiation, brand loyalty is fragile, making customer retention a constant and costly challenge.

4.5 Internal Assessment: Strengths and Vulnerabilities

A balanced view of eSewa's current position reveals market-leading strengths and specific areas requiring strategic attention.

Core Strengths

  • Market Leadership: A dominant position with 10 million registered users
  • Unmatched Network: Extensive reach with 450,000 merchants and 300,000 agents across all 77 districts
  • Pioneering Brand: High brand loyalty and trust built over 16 years as Nepal's first digital wallet
  • Sustained Profitability: A positive balance sheet for the past three years, a rare achievement in the sector

Identified Vulnerabilities

  • Dated UI/UX: A recognized need to modernize the user interface and experience to meet evolving user expectations
  • Eroding Brand Appeal in Youth Segment: While a core part of our user base, this demographic shows stronger affinity for Khalti's brand and UI/UX, posing a significant churn risk
  • Late Mover in Remittance: A recent entrant into the digital remittance space, a key strength of the newly merging IME Pay

This internal assessment, when viewed against the external pressures, narrows our viable strategic options and dictates a response that leverages our core strengths while mitigating our known vulnerabilities.

The competitive ground is shifting with the merger of its two largest rivals, creating a consolidated challenger with significant market power. The decision CEO Jagdish Khadka makes next will determine whether eSewa can defend its market dominance or will be forced to cede ground in the new landscape.

5.2 Framework Analysis of Options

Strategic Evaluation: This is a bold, offensive strategy to fundamentally expand eSewa's Scope and unlock powerful new Sources of Value Creation. By shifting the business model from transactions to long-term wealth management and SME credit, eSewa could become the primary financial institution for millions of Nepalis, creating deep, defensible customer relationships.

Through the 4S Framework:

  • Scope & Value Capture: Deepens the ecosystem by moving into core banking. Aims to capture significantly higher profit, with the potential for up to 10x higher profit per user than payments.
  • Risk: This move increases complexity and regulatory burden, testing the organization's Speed of adaptation in a new domain.

Option 2: Strengthen Core Digital Payments

Strategic Evaluation: This is a focused, defensive strategy aimed at reinforcing existing Scale and Scope. The objective is to protect the core business against the newly merged competitor by deepening market penetration in rural and youth segments, thereby defending eSewa's primary domain of leadership.

Through the 4S Framework:

  • Scale & Speed: Aims to grow the existing user base by 10% and defend market share by focusing on operational excellence and brand loyalty.
  • Risk: While reinforcing Scale, this safer option risks ceding the next frontier of Value Capture to more aggressive competitors and could lead to long-term strategic stagnation.

Option 3: Expand Internationally

Strategic Evaluation: This is a diversification strategy to achieve new geographic Scale and broaden the company's Scope beyond Nepal. eSewa could replicate its successful playbook in other cash-dominant emerging markets, establishing a first-mover advantage.

Through the 4S Framework:

  • Entire Framework: A play to establish new ecosystems by leveraging proven strategies for Scope (partnerships), Scale (agent networks), Speed (adaptation), and Value (first-mover advantage).
  • Risk: This is the highest-risk/highest-reward play, demanding a complete organizational commitment and carrying immense execution risk.

6.1 Three Imperatives for Digital Leadership

The central argument of this analysis is clear: sustained competitive advantage in the digital age is no longer a function of discrete products or services. It is achieved through the masterful orchestration of dynamic, value-creating ecosystems. The fusion of the 4S framework with eSewa's real-world success offers a powerful playbook for modern business leaders.

1. Think in Ecosystems, Not Industries

Leaders must look beyond the confines of their traditional industry to build and nurture value networks of partners, developers, and customers. This is the essence of mastering digital Scopeâ€”understanding that competitive advantage lies in the network you orchestrate, not just the products you build, as eSewa did with its 300,000 agents.

2. Build Platforms for Scale and Speed

Durable advantage is derived from digital platforms architected to enable network effects, facilitate rapid scaling, and provide the agility to adapt. Mastering digital Scale and Speed means creating platforms that connect participants in a virtuous cycle, allowing for exponential growth and strategic pivots as the market evolves.

3. Architect for Value Capture

Creating value is necessary but insufficient for success. Leaders must strategically design business models to control key digital points in the ecosystem to ensure long-term profitability. This is the art of Value Capture: building an indispensable platform that allows you to secure a disproportionate share of the value you help create.

Essential Lessons

  • Bridge the Digital Divide with a Human Touch: In emerging markets, a purely digital approach is insufficient. eSewa's agent network was a brilliant hybrid strategy that built the trust, access, and education necessary to onboard a cash-first society.
  • Build Moats, Not Just Apps: A single feature is easy to copy; a deeply integrated ecosystem is not. By weaving itself into the fabric of daily lifeâ€”from school fees to utility billsâ€”eSewa built a competitive advantage that is durable and difficult to replicate.
