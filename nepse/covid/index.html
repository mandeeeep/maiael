Executive Summary

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered unprecedented volatility in Nepal's stock market, revealing the profound influence of macroeconomic variables and behavioral biases on market movements. This analysis examines the complex interplay between fundamental economic factors and investor psychology during one of the most turbulent periods in Nepalese financial history.

Market Dynamics

  • Initial Crash: NEPSE index declined over 30% in March 2020
  • Record Recovery: Surged to 3,000+ points by late 2021
  • Monetary Easing: Policy rate cut from 5.0% to 3.0%

  • Loss Aversion: Strong reluctance to realize losses

Macroeconomic Variables and Stock Market Indices

Interest Rate Effects on NEPSE

Empirical research has established a robust negative relationship between interest rates and equity returns in Nepal's stock market. Studies employing Autoregressive Distributed Lag (ARDL) models confirm that higher lending rates exert substantial downward pressure on stock prices through multiple transmission channels [397, 427].

Key Empirical Findings:
  • Error correction term: -0.993882 (p = 0.000) indicating rapid adjustment
  • Policy rate cut from 5.0% to 3.0% during COVID-19
  • Long-run coefficient: -195.27 for WALR impact

Behavioral Biases and Investor Behavior

Herding Behavior During COVID-19

Theoretical Foundation

Herding behavior refers to the tendency of investors to mimic the actions of others rather than rely on private information. In retail-dominated markets like Nepal, informational cascades predominate as individual investors lack resources for independent fundamental analysis [496] [427].

Mechanisms:
  • • Informational cascades
  • • Reputational herding
  • • Social influence effects

Market Impact

Herding behavior created asymmetric patterns in Nepal's market, with stronger effects during bullish phases than bearish conditions [381].

Consequences:
  • • Price distortion and overshooting
  • • Volatility amplification
  • • Reduced market efficiency
  • • Systematic wealth transfers

Phase-Specific Manifestations

Crash Phase

Panic selling coordination with catalyst investors triggering cascading responses [516].

Mechanism: Social media amplification, brokerage observation of synchronized client actions
Bull Run Phase

FOMO effects driving retail investor surge "without any apparent fundamental reason" [502].

Mechanism: Social validation, fear of exclusion, observation of early entrants' gains
Correction Phase

Coordinated exit as momentum shifts induced herding in selling direction.

Mechanism: Risk of buying at peaks and panic-selling at losses

Overconfidence Bias

Empirical Evidence from Nepal

Research by Adhikari et al. (2025) provides direct empirical confirmation: overconfidence bias had the strongest influence on investment decisions (β = 0.421), with correlation to investment decisions (r = 0.700) highest among all behavioral factors [334].

Key Findings:
  • • Model explained 59.1% of variance
  • • Negative association with performance
  • • Strongest predictor among behavioral factors
Self-Attribution Mechanism

Successful early trades attributed to personal ability, while subsequent losses blamed on external factors—preserving overconfidence despite negative feedback.

Result: Asymmetric learning and bias reinforcement

Loss Aversion and Anchoring

Loss Aversion Foundation

Loss aversion—the asymmetric valuation of gains and losses with losses weighted approximately twice as heavily as equivalent gains—was profoundly activated during Nepal's COVID-19 market turmoil [503].

Survey Evidence:
  • • Mean agreement: 4.64/7 on loss aversion scale
  • • Strong agreement: 5.14/7 for asymmetric valuation
  • • Regret aversion: "feel pain of mental regret higher than joy"

Anchoring Bias

Anchoring bias—insufficient adjustment from initial reference points—complemented loss aversion by creating sticky price expectations and limit order clustering at psychological levels [528].

Common Anchors:
  • • Pre-pandemic index levels (~1,500 points)
  • • Personal purchase prices
  • • Recent high water marks (3,198 peak)
  • • Round numbers (1,000; 2,000; 3,000)
