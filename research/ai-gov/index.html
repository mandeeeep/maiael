Strategic Briefing: Nepal Rastra Bank Artificial Intelligence Guidelines

Executive Summary

The Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) has issued its comprehensive Artificial Intelligence Guidelines, fundamentally reshaping the regulatory environment for all licensed institutions (LIs). This development transitions Artificial Intelligence (AI) from a technological opportunity to a core strategic and compliance consideration, establishing a formal framework designed to balance the significant potential of AI innovation with the critical imperatives of financial stability, operational resilience, and consumer protection.

Core Strategic Objectives

  • Promoting Responsible Innovation: Drive AI adoption to enhance efficiency and customer experience within a framework that safeguards financial stability and operational resilience.
  • Ensuring Ethical and Fair Application: Mandate transparent, explainable, and accountable AI systems that uphold customer rights and prevent discriminatory outcomes.
  • Mitigating Emergent Risks: Establish proactive and holistic risk management to identify, govern, and mitigate AI-associated threats from model decay to cyber-attacks.
  • Fostering Financial Inclusion: Position AI as a strategic tool for expanding accessible and affordable financial services to all population segments.

These guidelines signal a fundamental shift by the NRB: AI is no longer a back-office efficiency tool but a board-level strategic concern directly tied to institutional reputation and financial stability.

1.0 Introduction: The New Regulatory Landscape for AI in Banking

The Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) has issued its comprehensive Artificial Intelligence Guidelines, fundamentally reshaping the regulatory environment for all licensed institutions (LIs). This move transitions Artificial Intelligence (AI) from a technological opportunity to a core strategic and compliance consideration.

The guidelines establish a formal framework designed to balance the significant potential of AI innovation with the critical imperatives of financial stability, operational resilience, and consumer protection. For leadership teams, understanding and strategically responding to these directives is no longer optional but essential for future growth and regulatory standing.

Regulatory Scope and Applicability

The guidelines apply broadly across Nepal's financial sector, covering:

Traditional Banking

  • Commercial Banks (Class A)
  • Development Banks (Class B)
  • Finance Companies (Class C)
  • Microfinance Institutions (Class D)

Digital Infrastructure

  • Nepal Infrastructure Bank Limited
  • Payment System Operators (PSOs)
  • Payment Service Providers (PSPs)

The breadth of applicability signals the NRB's intent for these principles to govern the entire digital financial ecosystem, covering applications in credit scoring, fraud detection, customer service, risk management, and compliance monitoring.

2.0 The Governance Mandate: Board and Senior Management Accountability

The NRB guidelines firmly establish that accountability for AI systems rests at the highest levels of the institution. The Board of Directors and Senior Management are designated as ultimately responsible for the outcomes and decisions generated by AI.

Leadership Responsibilities Matrix

Governing Body Mandated Responsibilities
Board of Directors • Defines the institution's risk tolerance for AI within the overall risk management framework
• Sets the strategic direction for AI adoption and deployment
• Establishes robust governance structures with clear roles and responsibilities
• Ensures implementation of ethical, transparent, and risk-aware practices
Senior Management • Ensures all AI usage aligns with strategic goals, risk appetite, and regulatory requirements
• Assesses and continuously monitors the institution's dependence on AI systems
• Oversees daily operation of AI systems within established frameworks
• Guarantees mechanisms for human oversight, auditability, and remediation

Figure 2.1: AI Governance Structure

Organizational hierarchy showing the flow of AI governance from Board of Directors through the AI Steering Committee to operational departments.

2.1 AI Strategy and Governance Framework

To operationalize this oversight, LIs are mandated to establish a comprehensive AI strategy and governance framework fully integrated with their overall risk management system. This requires the formation of a cross-disciplinary AI steering committee composed of senior management and key staff from business, risk, IT, legal, audit, and HR departments.

2.2 Third-Party AI Risk Management

The guidelines draw a critical strategic distinction between internal and external AI use:

Internal Use of Third-Party AI

Not considered outsourcing

  • AI tools for document drafting
  • Internal data analysis
  • Governed by internal policies

Outsourced AI Services

Subject to full outsourcing regulations

  • Third-party uses AI to deliver services
  • Requires Board approval
  • NRB notification mandatory

3.0 A Risk-Based Framework: Identifying and Managing AI Risks

Risk management serves as the central operational pillar of the new guidelines. The NRB mandates a proactive, risk-based approach, underscored by the introduction of a formal classification for "high-risk" AI systems.

3.1 High-Risk AI System Criteria

An AI system must be classified as "high-risk" if it meets one or more of the following criteria:

1. Serious Harm

Potential to cause significant financial loss, create legal liabilities, or result in denial of essential services to customers.

2. Broad Impact

Large-scale deployment increases possibility of systemic risk affecting multiple institutions or the financial sector.

3. Minimal Human Oversight

Operates with limited human supervision, increasing likelihood of unchecked errors or misuse.

4. Rights Risk

Poses direct risk to fundamental individual rights, including privacy, fairness, and non-discrimination.

5. Sensitive Data Use

Processes highly sensitive information such as biometric data or extensive personal datasets.

Figure 3.1: AI Risk Assessment Framework

Risk assessment flowchart illustrating the decision process for classifying AI systems as high-risk or standard, with corresponding control requirements.

3.2 Core Risk Management Domains

Integrated Risk Management

AI-related risks must be embedded into existing enterprise risk management and internal control frameworks. All identified risks, owners, mitigation measures, and monitoring mechanisms must be documented in a dedicated risk register section.

Model Risk Management

Formal model risk management practice covering the entire lifecycle from development and validation to monitoring and decommissioning. All AI models used in decision-making must be rigorously tested for accuracy, reliability, and fairness.

Data Governance

Robust data governance policies ensuring quality, integrity, and accuracy of training and operational data. Formal data retention policy defining collection, storage, usage, and disposal rules.

Cybersecurity

AI systems must be protected against cyber threats through robust security measures, regular audits, and compliance with NRB's Cyber Resilience Guidelines, 2023.

Ethical Use and Bias Mitigation

Proactive measures to identify and mitigate biases in AI algorithms, ensuring decisions do not discriminate against any individual or group.

4.0 Operationalizing Transparency, Fairness, and Data Protection

The guidelines translate high-level principles of ethics and fairness into concrete operational requirements governing customer interaction and data management.

4.1 Transparency and Explainability

LIs have direct obligations to make AI systems understandable to all stakeholders:

Explainable AI

Decision-making processes must be explainable to customers, regulators, and auditors. Any AI-generated content must be clearly labeled.

Customer Communication

Customers must be informed whenever AI systems are used in decisions affecting them. AI usage must be disclosed during general interactions.

Audit Trails

Comprehensive audit logs of AI decision-making processes must be maintained, recommended to align with ISO/IEC 42001 standards.

4.2 Data Privacy and Protection

The guidelines reinforce existing data protection laws while introducing AI-specific requirements:

Legal Compliance

Strict adherence to Nepal's Privacy Act, 2075 (2018)

Data Minimization

Collect only data necessary for intended AI applications

Explicit Consent

Obtain customer consent before using personal data in AI systems

Opt-Out Rights

Provide clear opt-out options without denial of essential services

4.3 Fairness and Non-Discrimination

Institutions must take proactive measures to ensure equitable AI outcomes:

  • Implement measures to identify and mitigate algorithmic biases
  • Conduct regular testing and monitoring for fairness
  • Obtain independent third-party validation for high-risk systems
  • Design inclusive systems serving all population segments

5.0 New Compliance and Reporting Obligations

The NRB has established explicit compliance and reporting mechanisms to create formal feedback loops with regulators.

5.1 Monitoring and Reporting

Continuous Monitoring

LIs must continuously monitor AI system performance. High-risk systems require dedicated monitoring plans.

Incident Reporting Protocol

Critical Incidents: Major system failures, data breaches, significant algorithmic bias. Report immediately per IT Guidelines, 2012 and Cyber Resilience Guidelines, 2023.
Non-Critical Incidents: Minor model errors, technical issues with minimal impact. Document internally and report quarterly.

5.2 Annual Regulatory Reporting

A mandatory annual report on all AI activities must be submitted to the NRB's concerned supervision department using the standardized template provided (Annex A). For AI systems classified as "not high-risk," institutions must maintain clear documentation justifying this classification.

AI practices must also adhere to key secondary regulations:

  • Cyber Resilience Guidelines, 2023
  • IT Guidelines, 2012
  • The Privacy Act, 2075 (2018)
  • Other relevant laws and regulations

6.0 Integrating AI Guidelines with Nepal's Broader Regulatory Framework

The NRB's AI Guidelines do not exist in isolation but are designed as integral components of Nepal's broader financial regulatory ecosystem.

6.1 Synergy with Cyber Resilience Guidelines, 2023

AI systems introduce new attack surfaces and vulnerabilities requiring extension of cyber resilience frameworks to address AI-specific threats:

  • Data Poisoning: Malicious corruption of training data
  • Model Evasion: Input manipulation to trick AI classifications
  • Model Theft: Unauthorized copying of proprietary AI models

6.2 Alignment with Privacy Act, 2075 (2018)

The AI guidelines' data protection requirements directly apply Privacy Act principles:

Consent Requirements

Explicit consent required for data collection and AI system usage (Section 12)

Purpose Limitation

Data collected for specific purposes can only be used for those purposes (Section 12.3)

Sensitive Information

Strict restrictions on processing sensitive data including caste, ethnicity, political affiliation (Section 27)

6.3 Foundational IT Controls

The IT Guidelines, 2012 remain the bedrock of technology governance, with AI-specific extensions:

  • Outsourcing management now explicitly evaluates AI capabilities
  • Business continuity planning accounts for AI service failures
  • IS Audits require assessment of AI model governance and algorithm bias

7.0 Strategic Framework for ISO/IEC 42001:2023 Certification

ISO/IEC 42001 represents the world's first international standard for Artificial Intelligence Management Systems (AIMS), providing a globally recognized framework for responsible AI governance.

7.1 Strategic Benefits of Certification

Building Trust and Transparency

Establishes formal commitment to transparent, trustworthy, and ethical AI systems foundational for stakeholder confidence.

Regulatory Compliance

Ensures alignment with emerging regulatory requirements, mitigating legal risks and demonstrating proactive governance.

Enhanced Risk Management

Implements systematic approach to identifying, assessing, and mitigating AI-related risks.

Competitive Advantage

Signals commitment to responsible AI management, enhancing brand reputation and building trust.

7.2 ISO 42001:2023 Detailed Framework for AI Governance

Core Components of ISO 42001 AIMS

  • Context and Leadership: Organizational AI strategy, governance policies, role definitions, and management commitment
  • Planning and Risk Management: Systematic AI risk assessment, impact evaluation, compliance mapping, and objective setting
  • Support and Resources: Competence management, resource allocation, communication strategies, and documentation control
  • Operation and Implementation: AI lifecycle management, supplier controls, change management, and incident response
  • Performance Evaluation: Continuous monitoring, internal audits, management reviews, and performance indicators
  • Improvement and Enhancement: Nonconformity management, corrective actions, and continual improvement processes

NRB Requirements ↔ ISO 42001 Implementation

Board Oversight: Define clear AI governance roles and senior leadership accountability

Risk Assessment: Systematic AI risk identification, analysis, and mitigation processes

Data Governance: Data lifecycle management covering privacy, security, and quality controls

Operational Controls: Structured procedures for AI development, testing, deployment, and monitoring

Continuous Monitoring: Systematic performance monitoring and improvement processes

Incident Management: Formal incident response with root cause analysis and corrective actions

Implementation Benefits for NRB Compliance

  • Regulatory Alignment: Structured approach to meet NRB guideline requirements systematically
  • Risk Mitigation: Systematic risk management reduces AI-related operational and compliance risks
  • Stakeholder Confidence: Demonstrates commitment to responsible AI governance and regulatory compliance
  • Operational Efficiency: Standardized processes improve efficiency and reduce governance overhead
  • Competitive Advantage: Positions organizations as leaders in responsible AI governance

PDCA = Plan → Do → Check → Act (Implementation Framework)

The detailed PDCA cycle above shows the comprehensive implementation approach required for Nepal Rastra Bank AI guidelines compliance:

  • PLAN: Strategic foundation with governance framework, risk assessment, impact analysis, and measurable objectives
  • DO: Resource deployment including team allocation, training programs, communication strategy, and AI system implementation

Why PDCA matters for NRB: The guidelines require "continuous monitoring" - this detailed PDCA framework provides the systematic, implementation-focused approach to achieve compliance. Each phase contains specific deliverables that align with regulatory requirements, ensuring both immediate compliance and long-term governance excellence.

7.3 Integration with Existing Management Systems

ISO 42001's design as a Management System standard enables efficient integration with existing frameworks:

Risk Assessment Extension

Augment existing risk registers to include AI-specific risks such as bias, transparency, and societal impact.

Policy Integration

Expand organizational policies to include dedicated AI policy aligned with strategic direction.

Audit Program Extension

Incorporate AIMS into established internal audit programmes assessing conformity with both organizational and ISO 42001 requirements.

Continual Improvement

Integrate AIMS nonconformities into existing corrective action processes for systematic improvement.

7.4 The Five Support Pillars

ISO 42001 identifies five essential pillars for trustworthy AI systems:

1. Resources

Financial, technological, human, and informational resources necessary for AIMS effectiveness.

2. Competence

Personnel competence through education, training, or experience in AI-related roles.

3. Awareness

Organizational awareness of AI policy, AIMS benefits, and consequences of non-conformity.

4. Communication

Systematic communication planning for internal and external AIMS stakeholders.

5. Documented Information

Comprehensive documentation control for policies, assessments, and performance records.

8.0 Strategic Imperatives and Implementation Roadmap

The NRB's guidelines present both strategic challenges and significant opportunities. Institutions viewing these as mere compliance burdens will face mounting regulatory scrutiny, while those embedding principles into core strategy will build trust and achieve sustainable innovation.

8.1 Immediate Action Items for Leadership

1. Establish Formal Governance

Form the mandated cross-disciplinary AI steering committee and develop the institutional AI strategy and governance framework for board approval.

2. Conduct AI System Inventory

Initiate comprehensive audit to identify all current and planned AI systems, classifying each as "high-risk" or "not high-risk" per NRB criteria.

3. Review and Align Policies

Audit and update existing risk management, cybersecurity, and data privacy policies to align with new AI guidelines.

4. Develop Reporting Framework

Implement internal processes and data collection mechanisms for mandatory annual AI report requirements.

5. Resource Capacity Building

Develop training programs on AI risks, technologies, and regulations for board members, senior management, and operational staff.

6. Enhance Customer Mechanisms

Review customer communication materials and grievance handling processes to address AI-driven decisions.

8.2 ISO/IEC 42001 Certification Roadmap

Phase 1: Foundation

Scope definition, AI risk and impact assessment, organizational context analysis.

Phase 2: Documentation

Develop AIMS documentation, AI policy, procedures, objectives, and Statement of Applicability.

Phase 3: Stage I Audit

External certification body conducts thorough documentation review for ISO 42001 compliance.

Phase 4: Stage II Audit

Evaluate practical implementation and operational effectiveness through hands-on assessment.

Phase 5: Certification

Receive ISO 42001 certification with ongoing annual surveillance audits for maintenance.

8.3 Strategic Recommendations

Based on comprehensive analysis, institutions should consider the following strategic approaches:

  • Phased Implementation: Propose "crawl-walk-run" approach for sustainable capability building
  • Industry Collaboration: Establish standardized industry forum for sharing best practices
  • Regulatory Engagement: Provide constructive feedback to NRB during consultative phase
  • Competitive Positioning: Transform compliance adherence into market differentiator
